Imagine spending two years and $3 million making a music video. Not a film. Not a mini-series. A music video — nearly seven minutes long, shot across Paris and Taipei, filled with 30 hidden references to the greatest paintings in art history, and produced with the same visual effects studio that brought Middle-earth and Pandora to life.

That's exactly what Jay Chou did. And the world noticed.

Two days after the release of *Children of the Sun* — Jay Chou's 16th studio album and his first in nearly four years — the lead single's music video is trending across 36 countries, generating over 6 million searches at a Google Trends temperature of 75. The numbers are significant. The story behind them is more so.

## A Seven-Minute Spectacle Four Years in the Making

Jay Chou last released an album in July 2022. For an artist of his stature — widely considered the most influential figure in Chinese-language pop music for over two decades — four years is an eternity. Fans waited. Streaming platforms waited. The entire Mandopop industry waited.

What arrived on March 25, 2026 was not just an album. It was a statement.

*Children of the Sun* (太陽之子) arrived with a music video that doubles as a short film. Filmed over two-plus years in Paris and Taipei, the production budget sat between US$2.8 million and $3.13 million — approximately NT$100 million. Pre-orders reportedly surpassed hundreds of thousands of copies before a single note dropped publicly.

For context: most major label music videos cost between $50,000 and $500,000. Jay Chou spent six times the upper end of that range, and the result looks every bit of it.

## When Wētā Workshop Meets Mandopop

The production partner on this project is the detail that stops people mid-scroll: **Wētā Workshop**, the legendary New Zealand visual effects and physical effects studio behind *The Lord of the Rings*, *Avatar*, *Planet of the Apes*, and *Dune*.

Wētā Workshop doesn't make music videos. Except now, apparently, they do — if you're Jay Chou.

The nearly seven-minute runtime unfolds as a gallery in motion. Embedded throughout are 30 references to iconic artworks: Gustav Klimt's gold-leaf figures, Edward Hopper's solitary interiors, Vincent van Gogh's swirling light, Jacques-Louis David's neoclassical grandeur. The references aren't decoration — they're architecture. Each frame is constructed to echo a specific painting, making the video as rewarding to pause and study as it is to watch straight through.

The artistic ambition here is rare in pop music at any level, let alone in Mandopop. It signals that Jay Chou isn't merely returning — he's repositioning what a Mandopop release can look and feel like in 2026.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is Jay Chou's new album *Children of the Sun*?**
*Children of the Sun* (太陽之子) is Jay Chou's 16th studio album, released digitally on March 25, 2026, with physical copies scheduled for April 10. It is his first album since July 2022 and represents a nearly four-year creative gap for one of Mandopop's defining artists.

**Who is Jay Chou and why is he significant?**
Jay Chou is a Taiwanese singer-songwriter, record producer, actor, and director widely regarded as the most influential figure in Chinese-language pop music over the past two decades. His genre-blending approach — fusing Western classical music, hip-hop, R&B, and traditional Chinese instruments — defined a generation of Mandopop and inspired countless artists across Asia.

**What makes the *Children of the Sun* music video unusual?**
The video was produced in collaboration with Wētā Workshop — the New Zealand studio behind *The Lord of the Rings*, *Avatar*, and *Dune* — filmed over more than two years in Paris and Taipei, with a budget of approximately US$3 million. At nearly seven minutes, it contains 30 hidden references to famous paintings including works by Klimt, Van Gogh, Hopper, and David.

**Why did Jay Chou wait nearly four years between albums?**
Jay Chou has not given a detailed public explanation for the gap. The production timeline of the *Children of the Sun* music video — over two years of filming — suggests at least part of the hiatus was devoted to this singular creative project.

**Why is this trending across 36 countries so quickly?**
The combination of factors is unusual: a globally recognized artist returning after a long absence, a visually unprecedented music video, the strategic timing against the rise of China as a top-four recorded music market, and the large, engaged Chinese diaspora communities in Malaysia, Singapore, Taiwan, and Hong Kong who amplify releases through dense social networks.
